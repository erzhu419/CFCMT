\section{Results}

\subsection{Strict leave-one-city-out dynamics}

\Cref{fig:strict-loo,tab:strict-loo} show the primary dynamics result.
Unweighted \ours beats the dense \Htwo-style residual baseline on all four held-out cities, with mean total-MSE ratio $0.759$.
Similarity-weighted \ours also wins on all four targets and improves the mean ratio to $0.578$.
The largest gains occur for Austin, Halifax, and MBTA.
Singapore is the hardest target: unweighted \ours is only slightly better than \Htwo, and similarity weighting is slightly worse than unweighted \ours while still remaining below the \Htwo baseline.

\subsection{Policy-level validation}

The strict one-step policy summary is more mixed but still favorable.
\ours improves reward/regret on three of four targets, improves mean absolute headway error on three of four, and lowers bunching rate on all four targets.
The mean regret ratio is $0.876$, and the mean bunching-rate ratio is $0.644$.
Halifax is the main policy exception: \ours lowers headway error slightly but has worse reward/regret than \Htwo.
This is consistent with a limited policy claim: mechanism-factored transfer improves cross-city dynamics strongly and often improves one-step policy quality, but it is not a universal policy dominance result.

\subsection{Source weighting is stable under a small grid}

The default source weighting uses $T=1.0$ and $\lambda=0.05$.
Across the $3 \times 3$ grid, all nine settings beat \Htwo on all six configured cross-city splits.
The mean weighted-\ours/\Htwo total-MSE ratio ranges only from $0.6025$ to $0.6149$.
The best setting in the grid is $T=0.5,\lambda=0$, but the default is not selected by post-hoc optimization and remains within the same narrow band.

\subsection{Calibration budget and mechanism-level errors}

The target-route calibration-budget sweep tests whether a calibrated dense residual baseline quickly erases the zero-calibration \ours advantage.
At $0\%$ target calibration, weighted \ours has mean ratio $0.578$ against the dense source-only baseline.
When $1\%$, $5\%$, $10\%$, and $25\%$ of target routes are added to the dense baseline, the uncalibrated weighted-\ours/H2O ratio remains below $1$ on three of four strict targets, with mean ratios $0.581$, $0.586$, $0.617$, and $0.677$, respectively.
The $100\%$ target-route point is an in-sample oracle reference and should not be interpreted as deployable zero-calibration evidence.

\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.70\textwidth]{calibration_budget_ratio.png}

\label{fig:calibration}

Per-mechanism errors show that the total-MSE advantage is not driven by one output group only.
Weighted \ours improves all three mechanism groups on average: headway/gap ratio $0.574$, demand/dwell/reward ratio $0.771$, and speed ratio $0.668$.
Headway/gap is the largest contributor to the main gain, which is consistent with the holding-control objective.

\subsection{Robustness and boundary conditions}

Generator robustness supports the main trend but also identifies failure modes.
Weighted \ours beats \Htwo on every city target in five of seven perturbation/noise scenarios.
Under headway-gap bias and mixed-mechanism bias, the result weakens to three of four targets.
Thus the evidence does not say that \ours is invariant to any target-generation change; it says that the result is robust to several plausible perturbations.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{generator_robustness.png}
\caption{Generator-bias and measurement-noise robustness under strict leave-one-city-out evaluation.}
\label{fig:generator}
\end{figure}

Mechanism ablations support the mechanism-specific interpretation, with a caveat.
Wrong mechanism groupings and action/time-only features are much worse than \Htwo, with mean ratios above $7$.
Dense matched sparse features are close to full \ours, with mean ratio $0.772$, which means the result should be described as evidence for mechanism-wise sparse transfer rather than definitive causal identification.

The source-subset check is the strongest limitation.
With all four cities, weighted \ours has mean ratio $0.578$.
Excluding Austin remains favorable with mean ratio $0.663$.
Excluding Singapore makes the result nearly tied with \Htwo, with mean ratio $0.998$.
The two-city Halifax-MBTA pair is slightly worse than \Htwo, with ratio $1.042$.
This indicates that source diversity matters: the method should be framed as multi-source cross-city transfer, not as guaranteed transfer between every pair of cities.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{source_subset_robustness.png}
\caption{Source-subset robustness. Removing data-source families reveals where the claim becomes weak.}
\label{fig:subset}
\end{figure}

Route-level bootstrap confidence intervals reinforce the same boundary.
Unweighted \ours has a route-bootstrap ratio confidence interval below $1$ for all four targets.
Weighted \ours has intervals below $1$ for Austin, Halifax, and MBTA, but Singapore crosses parity with interval $[0.993,1.002]$.
The conservative reading is that source weighting improves the mean strongly, while Singapore remains the least secure target.

\subsection{Auxiliary sampled rollout}

The sampled BusSimEnv rollout is not primary evidence, but it checks whether the learned one-step action preferences are grossly inconsistent with executable line environments.
Under a fair seeded rollout over ten Austin/Halifax line episodes, weighted \ours reaches parity with the dense policy: mean reward is $-1121.574$ for weighted \ours and $-1121.612$ for \Htwo.
The headway-error metric is slightly worse for weighted \ours, $588.2$ seconds versus $585.6$ seconds.
Thus rollout supports a limited claim: after schema and reward alignment, \ours does not collapse in executable simulation, but the closed-loop advantage remains too small to serve as the main evidence.
